> General guidance for staff drafting client replies. Figures and thresholds are
> indicative and current as at the 2025–26 financial year — always confirm the
> latest rates and a client's specific circumstances before giving formal advice.
> Nothing here is personal financial advice on its own.

## SMSF — extra tax on balances over $3 million (Division 296)

From the 2025–26 income year, a new measure (commonly called **Division 296**)
applies an **additional 15% tax** on the portion of superannuation earnings that
relates to a member's **total superannuation balance above $3 million**.

Key points to communicate:
- It is an **extra 15% on top of** the existing 15% tax on fund earnings, so the
  effective rate on the affected portion is around **30%**.
- It applies **only to the proportion of earnings attributable to the balance
  over $3 million**, not the whole balance. If a member is just over $3m, only a
  small slice is affected.
- "Earnings" for this measure is calculated on the **movement in total super
  balance** across the year, which can include **unrealised gains** (paper gains
  on assets not yet sold).
- The $3 million threshold is **per member**, not per fund. In a two-member fund,
  each member is assessed against their own balance.
- The tax is assessed to the **individual member** (who can choose to pay it
  personally or from their super), not to the fund directly.

Typical client reply: reassure them that only the portion above $3m is affected,
that we will model their specific position, and that planning options (e.g.
contribution timing, balance equalisation between spouses) may help.

## Super contribution caps (2024–25 / 2025–26)

- **Concessional (before-tax) cap:** $30,000 per year. Includes employer SG,
  salary sacrifice, and personal deductible contributions.
- **Non-concessional (after-tax) cap:** $120,000 per year.
- **Bring-forward rule:** under age 75 and eligible, members can bring forward up
  to **3 years** of non-concessional caps ($360,000) in one year.
- **Carry-forward concessional:** unused concessional cap from the prior 5 years
  can be used if the total super balance was under $500,000 at 30 June prior.
- Exceeding caps can trigger extra tax — flag for review before large
  contributions.

## SMSF related parties — leasing, NALI and NALE

SMSFs must deal with related parties **on arm's-length (market) terms**.

- Renting a fund-owned property to a related party (e.g. a member's child's
  business) **below market rent** is a problem: the shortfall can make the rental
  income **Non-Arm's-Length Income (NALI)**, taxed at the **top rate of 45%**
  rather than 15%.
- Similarly, **Non-Arm's-Length Expenses (NALE)** — paying too little for a
  service to the fund — can taint income.
- **Business real property** (genuine commercial premises) **can** be leased to a
  related party, but **only at full market rent** with a proper lease in place.
- Residential property generally **cannot** be leased to related parties at all.

Typical client reply: leasing the commercial unit to a relative's business is
fine **only at market rent** with a written lease; charging below market risks
the income being taxed at 45%. Recommend getting a market rent appraisal and
documenting the lease. This is a compliance risk — flag for partner review.

## Capital gains tax on selling an investment property

- CGT is the **capital gain** (sale proceeds less the **cost base**) added to
  assessable income in the year of the **contract (sale) date**.
- **Cost base** includes the purchase price plus buying/selling costs (stamp
  duty, legal, agent commission) and certain capital improvements.
- **50% CGT discount:** individuals who have held the asset **more than 12
  months** generally get a **50% discount** on the gain.
- **Main residence exemption:** the family home is generally exempt; the **6-year
  rule** can keep a former home exempt while rented for up to 6 years in some
  cases.
- Capital **losses** offset capital gains (this year or carried forward).

Typical client reply: for a rental held ~6 years, the gain is proceeds minus the
cost base, with the 50% discount applying. Recommend gathering purchase records,
improvement receipts, and sale costs so we can estimate the gain before they sell.

## Division 7A — company loans to shareholders or associates

If a **private company lends money to a shareholder or associate** (or pays a
personal expense for them), **Division 7A** can treat it as an **unfranked
deemed dividend** — taxable to the individual — unless it is managed correctly.

To avoid a deemed dividend, before the company's lodgement day either:
- **repay** the loan, or
- put it on a **complying Division 7A loan agreement** (written, with a minimum
  yearly repayment and interest at the ATO **benchmark rate**; max 7-year term
  unsecured).

Typical client reply: the company can lend the $25k, but it must be on a
complying written loan agreement with minimum repayments and benchmark interest,
or repaid before lodgement — otherwise it's taxed as a dividend in the
shareholder's hands. We can set up the agreement.

## Instant asset write-off and small business depreciation

- Eligible **small businesses** can **immediately deduct** the cost of eligible
  depreciating assets under the **instant asset write-off** threshold, in the
  year the asset is **first used or installed ready for use**.
- The threshold is **per asset** and changes year to year — **confirm the current
  threshold** before advising (it has commonly been around **$20,000** for recent
  years; assets above the threshold are depreciated in the small business pool).
- The asset must be used for business purposes; **GST-registered** businesses
  claim the **GST-exclusive** cost.
- Timing matters: it must be **installed ready for use by 30 June** to claim in
  this financial year.

Typical client reply: a ~$40k dental chair is likely **above** the instant
write-off threshold, so it would usually be **depreciated** rather than written
off immediately — but confirm the current threshold and eligibility. To claim
this year it must be installed and ready for use by 30 June.
